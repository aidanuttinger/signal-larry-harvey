The Industry's Problem

An estate plan transfers assets.
A legacy plan transfers values.

Most families have the first. Almost none have the second. The financial services industry has spent decades perfecting the machinery of wealth transfer — the trusts, the tax strategies, the account structures — while treating the question of what it's all for as something soft. Optional. Outside the scope.

It isn't optional. It's the entire point. When wealth changes hands without values, it dissolves into conflict, aimlessness, or the quiet tragedy of money that meant something to one generation and nothing to the next.

The Formation

In 1985, a young man from Tampa
walked into financial services —
not chasing money.

What Larry Harvey was chasing was harder to name. He was 23 years old, and he already knew, somehow, that wealth — managed well — could do something extraordinary. Not just grow. Not just protect. He'd seen what happened when it didn't: families fractured over estates, money that meant everything to a founder and nothing to a son, charitable intentions that died with the person who held them.

"I wanted to build a firm where I could impact as many lives as possible — not just manage their money, but honor what it represents."

His formation began at Lutheran Brotherhood — a faith-based financial institution where the question of money's meaning wasn't metaphysical. It was structural. It was how the firm operated. Larry rose to management, becoming Estate and Charitable Planning Specialist for the entire southeast region. He wasn't learning to manage wealth. He was learning to honor it.

By 1998, when he founded Harvey Financial Group, he had thirteen years of evidence that wealth managed without values is wealth that eventually costs you everything that matters. When HFG Wealth Management launched in 2008, it was with 23 years of formation behind it — and one question at the center: What is this wealth for?

That question has never changed. It has been asked in good markets and bad ones. In times of abundance and times of loss. In conversations with patriarchs who built their wealth from nothing, and with second-generation families who inherited more than they expected. The question is the same because the answer is always different — and always the most important thing in the room.

35 years later, HFG Wealth Management serves families across three Texas locations — The Woodlands, Houston, and beyond — as a Legacy Architecture firm. Not a wealth management firm that happens to be faith-based. A legacy firm that happens to manage wealth. That distinction — held quietly for 35 years — is the single most powerful market differentiator in the industry today.
