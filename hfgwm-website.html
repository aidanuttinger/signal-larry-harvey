What wealth management gets wrong

14,000 firms will manage your assets.
Almost none will ask what they're for.

"The financial services industry measures everything in basis points. The relationship is transactional. The horizon is quarterly. And the question nobody asks is the only one that matters."
Affluent families sit across the table from advisors who are technically competent and spiritually absent. Advisors who can tell you exactly how your portfolio performed last quarter, but cannot tell you what it's for. Advisors who optimize returns without ever asking what the return is meant to return to.
The result is wealth without purpose. Financial success without grounding. A perfect plan for a life that was never fully examined. This is the problem Larry Harvey recognized 35 years ago — before it had a name. It's the problem HFG was built to solve.
And in a world where $84 trillion is about to change hands between generations, the stakes have never been higher. Research is unambiguous: 72% of heirs fire their parents' advisor within a year of inheritance. Not because the returns were bad. Because the relationship never went deep enough to survive the person it was built around.

The Category Problem
"Wealth management" is a commodity category. One of 14,000+ registered investment advisors in the United States. Every conversation starts with performance, fees, and fund comparisons. Differentiation is nearly impossible — because the category was designed to commoditize.
02
The Depth Problem
Most advisors are great at one thing — investments, or tax, or estate planning — but never the whole picture. And never with the depth of a relationship that feels like a trusted family advisor rather than a service provider. The right families deserve both.
03
The Values Problem
For families who believe wealth is a stewardship — not just an asset — the standard advisor relationship feels hollow. There's no space for questions about purpose, giving, or what your wealth is actually meant to accomplish in the world.
04
The Legacy Problem
An estate plan transfers assets. A legacy plan transfers values. Most families have the first and are missing the second. The most enduring legacies are measured not by what was accumulated, but by what was given — and who inherited the wisdom to steward it.

The origin story
that explains everything

"I started in this industry believing the same thing everyone else did — that our job was to maximize returns. It took me years to realize I was answering the wrong question."
— Larry Harvey, Founder, HFG Wealth Management
Larry Harvey began his career in financial services in 1985. He learned the craft the old-fashioned way — sitting across tables from families who had built real wealth, listening to what they worried about at night, and slowly realizing that the conversations that mattered most had almost nothing to do with portfolio allocations.
The moment that changed everything: a client with a perfectly structured estate plan looked across the table and asked, "What does any of this actually mean? What am I leaving my kids besides money?" That question haunted Larry for years. He founded HFG to answer it. Not as a tagline. As a practice.
HFG's foundation is faith — not as a marketing angle, but as a conviction that wealth is entrusted rather than owned, that stewardship matters, and that the most enduring legacies are measured by what was given. Today, HFG serves multi-generational families across three Texas locations. Families stay across generations not because of investment performance alone, but because HFG is one of the only firms in Texas that asks what your wealth is actually for.
